The decision

Custom at template price

Simple + cash now

One tier = less work for two people on the side. One-time = bulk cash up front, no churn or recurring to babysit. The brand stays “eenmalig, van jou.”

The reasoning

Why one price, and why one-time

Three forces point the same way — toward a single low one-time number rather than a tier ladder or a subscription.

  • Your customer can’t comfortably pay €1k. 81% of NL hair businesses are solo/ZZP — ~€33k revenue, ~€21k profit. At €999 a website is 3% of revenue / ~2.4 weeks of profit, and they anchor “website” to free Instagram or a €375–495 template — not to a €2.500 agency. On a cold approach, €1k is above where they’ll sign. €495 is ~1,5% of revenue / ~1 week of profit — the side of the line they’ll actually cross.
  • Near-zero cost means €495 is still pure margin. The mockup costs ~€0.05, hosting is ~€0 marginal, so the only real input is your build time. You’re not discounting into a loss — you’re pricing to what converts, and at €495 the whole sum is contribution.
  • One-time + one tier fits who you are right now. Two people on the side want bulk cash up front, not a recurring book to manage, support, and chase churn on. And one tier is simply less operational work than a good/better/best ladder — which also keeps the brand’s “eenmalig, geen verrassingen” promise intact.

Price to what a cash-thin salon will actually sign for — not to what the work is “worth” against an agency.

— affordability and conversion beat value-capture while the funnel is unproven

The one honest trade-off: because you’re capacity-bound (~50–100 builds/year, ever) at near-zero cost, a lower price means lower revenue per slot — €495 is roughly half the €/build of €999. But the first phase is proof + learning, where the binding problem is conversion, not margin. A price that gets a cash-thin salon to yes beats a higher one that stalls the funnel. €495 is the right call for where you are; there’s room to revisit it upward once the funnel and testimonials prove out (see §06).

€495 leaves money on the table versus the value delivered (you’re ~5–10× below an agency). That’s deliberate: while the cold funnel is unproven and you want first customers + testimonials, conversion is worth more than margin. Once the funnel demonstrably fills and you have proof, you can test the one-time upward (§06) — but you start where salons say yes.

The structure

The offer, in detail

The headline — €495 eenmalig (push this)

One price, one tier, alles inbegrepen: a fully custom site, mobile-responsive, your Treatwell/booking link embedded, Google Maps, basic SEO, SSL, handover. The thing they’ve already seen as a mockup, made real and put live. This is the offer in the opener and on the call — no menu, no “vanaf”, no surprises.

Optional add-on — wijzigingen / onderhoud (as you already had)

An optional care plan for ongoing changes + hosting, ~€10–15/mo or €149/jr vooruitbetaald — explicitly not a support retainer. The economics are excellent: infra is ~€0 marginal (Vercel Pro is billed per seat, not per project; Netlify Free permits commercial use; SSL is free), so the only hard cost is the .nl renewal (~€16,50/jr) → ~88% cash margin. The one rule: cap included edits (≈2/jr) and bill extra hourly, so it never eats the build time that actually caps the business.

Alternative payment model — €20–79/mo subscription (offered, not pushed)

For owners who balk at any upfront sum, offer a monthly subscription instead of the €495 — the model PlazaXL and StudioLee already prove converts cash-thin salons. But you don’t lead with it. Right now you prefer the one-time: two people on the side want bulk cash now, not a recurring book to service and chase churn on. Keep the subscription as a fallback for a “can’t do €495 at once” objection, and set the exact monthly point (somewhere in €20–79) only when you actually need it.

▸ Net

Lead with €495 one-time, everywhere. Care plan is a quiet optional add-on; the subscription is a quiet fallback. One number does ~95% of the work — which is exactly the operational simplicity a two-person side project needs.

Guardrails

What not to do

✕ Avoid
  • Don’t add tiers “to be safe.” A good/better/best ladder is more work to build, quote and maintain, and the lift is modest. One clean price is the right call for two people on the side.
  • Don’t drift below ~€450. Too close to the €375–399 template farms and you forfeit the “custom, not template” perception that justifies choosing you at all.
  • Don’t discount reflexively off €495. A fast drop tells a Dutch buyer the price was fake. Prefer the honest monthly-subscription option, or a true founding-customer rate, over knocking euros off.
  • Don’t make the care plan or subscription mandatory, and don’t let the care plan creep into open-ended support — it would tax the build capacity that caps everything.
  • Don’t sell €495 as “marketing spend” (real salon marketing budgets are tiny). Frame it as a one-time, owned asset — “je eigen site, eenmalig, van jou.”
  • Don’t anchor against a Treatwell-commission saving — there is none (the site embeds Treatwell). Anchor against agency quotes and perpetual DIY/competitor subscriptions.
Measure, then revisit

Open questions — what to measure

  1. Does €495 convert on cold outreach — can it fill ~1–2 builds/week? The core number. If yes, the price is right; if the funnel overflows, that’s your signal there’s room to test higher.
  2. How many balk at €495 upfront but would take the €20–79/mo subscription? Tells you whether to surface the monthly option earlier, and where to set it.
  3. Care-plan attach rate + real edit-hours per customer/year? Validates the €10–15/mo passive framing and whether the small recurring is worth servicing.
  4. Real build-hours per site? Turns “€495 is all margin” into a measured €-per-operator-hour — the true denominator for the side-project’s payoff.
  5. Once you have ~5–10 sales + testimonials, does a €595–795 test hold conversion? The proof and the social proof are what would let you lift the one-time later, if you want to.

Provenance

How we got here & what the fact-check changed

Six web-grounded research dimensions (salon economics · agency/freelancer prices · the DIY/SaaS floor · direct productized competitors · pricing model & psychology · maintenance/hosting norms), each adversarially fact-checked against independent sources. The recommendation moved as the evidence and the operator’s own read came in:

  • From €999 → €495. The research first framed €999 as “underpriced vs an agency” — true on the value axis, but the average solo salon (€33k revenue) won’t pay €1k on a cold approach, and the salon market visibly clears at €375–795. Affordability + conversion win while the funnel is unproven.
  • From a 3-tier ladder → one price. Tiers add build/quote/maintenance work and the lift is modest (the famous “decoy” effect needs a dominated same-price option, which a Pro tier isn’t — a higher tier only triggers the weaker ~50–60% “compromise” pull). One clean price suits a two-person side project and the “geen verrassingen” brand.
  • From recurring-as-lever → one-time-preferred. A subscription is the strongest affordability tool, but it imposes churn/support on a fixed-capacity team. With two people doing this on the side wanting bulk cash now, one-time is correct; the subscription stays a quiet fallback.
  • Caveats the fact-check flagged: the ZZP net-profit figure is single-source (revenue is primary-confirmed); “charm pricing +24–35%” is one retail experiment maxing at ~31%; Fresha’s free tier appears gone.
